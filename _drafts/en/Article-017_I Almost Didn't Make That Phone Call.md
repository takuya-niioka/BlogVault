# I Almost Didn't Make That Phone Call

*What 9 years in automotive safety taught me about the real cost of procrastination*

---

I work in automotive safety systems at a major Japanese OEM. For nine years, I've been responsible for ADAS functions — the systems that help drivers avoid accidents.

Today, I almost made a mistake that would have cost us weeks.

Not a technical mistake. An avoidance mistake.

---

## The situation

We were navigating a regulatory ambiguity. Our team had been operating on an assumption about which organization owned a critical compliance judgment — the certification department or the development team.

I had a nagging feeling the assumption was wrong. But I wasn't certain. And making the call felt uncomfortable. What if I was wrong and wasting everyone's time? What if it raised questions I wasn't ready to answer?

So I did the mental math that engineers are actually very good at:

**Cost of calling now:** 5 minutes, possible awkwardness, uncertainty.

**Cost of not calling:** …I didn't actually calculate this one.

That's the mistake most of us make. We calculate the cost of acting. We forget to calculate the cost of not acting.

---

## What happened when I made the call

I called the certification engineer.

In five minutes, I discovered we had a fundamental misunderstanding. Our team assumed the certification department would own the compliance judgment. They assumed we would.

Nobody owned it.

I could have stopped there. Processed the finding. Scheduled a follow-up. Instead, I walked directly to the powertrain controls engineer to test the new understanding against another part of our system.

Another critical misalignment, uncovered in ten minutes.

Here's what happened next:

- Built a decision analysis with three options
- Escalated to management
- Direction confirmed in ten minutes

Total time from the first phone call to resolved direction: under two hours.

---

## The structure underneath

This wasn't luck. And it wasn't unusual speed.

It was the result of accurate cost accounting.

Most engineers I know — including myself for most of my career — systematically underestimate the cost of inaction. We frame delay as "gathering more information" or "waiting until I'm sure."

But inaction has a cost that compounds silently:

- Misalignments become load-bearing assumptions
- Wrong directions accumulate work
- The longer you wait, the more expensive the correction

The engineers who move fast aren't more confident. They're more accurate at calculating what *not* acting costs.

When I did that calculation honestly — 5 minutes of discomfort now versus potentially weeks of wrong direction later — the answer was obvious.

---

## The principle

Good engineering is often described in technical terms: modeling, analysis, precision. But there's a behavioral layer underneath that rarely gets discussed.

The engineers who consistently produce good outcomes tend to share one habit:

They make the uncomfortable call before they're sure they need to.

Not because they're reckless. Because they've learned to calculate inaction cost accurately.

That calculation changes everything. A 5-minute call stops being "unnecessary overhead" and becomes "the cheapest possible insurance against weeks of wrong direction."

---

## One question to close

What's the "I'll deal with it when I'm sure" item sitting in your work right now?

What would actually happen if you made that call today — not because you're certain, but because you've honestly calculated what it costs not to?
